\chapter{Literature Review}
\section{Introduction}

Large Language Models (LLMs) have fundamentally transformed natural language processing by demonstrating unprecedented fluency and reasoning capabilities. However, their deployment in knowledge-intensive domains, where factual reliability is a strict prerequisite rather than a desirable trait, remains severely constrained. In contexts such as automated reasoning, medical diagnostics, and structured data querying, AI systems must do more than approximate plausible answers; they must ground their outputs in deterministically verifiable knowledge.

The foundational challenge hindering this adoption is hallucination: the generation of fluent but factually incorrect or unsupported claims. Because these models generate text autoregressively over an unconstrained vocabulary, they lack a built-in mechanism to ensure structural faithfulness to an external factual source. While current mitigation strategies, including chain-of-thought prompting and soft retrieval augmentation, significantly improve empirical accuracy, they fundamentally fail to provide hard, mathematical guarantees against hallucinated reasoning steps.

To address these challenges, this chapter examines existing research on these vulnerabilities and explores the progression of constrained decoding architectures designed to solve them. By doing so, it establishes the theoretical foundations for the Dynamic Context-Aware Trie (DCA-Trie). The review is structured around nine thematic pillars. First, Sections~\ref{sec:hallucination} and~\ref{sec:cot} formalize the problem by diagnosing hallucination's structural causes and exposing the faithfulness ceiling of unconstrained multi-step reasoning. Section~\ref{sec:kgqa} establishes the technical domain in which DCA-Trie operates: knowledge graph question answering and the mechanics of multi-hop inference. Sections~\ref{sec:constrained} and~\ref{sec:beam} form the methodological core, tracing the evolution of constrained decoding paradigms, from foundational logit masking to leading graph-specific implementations like GCR and DoG, and analyzing their interaction with beam search. Section~\ref{sec:rag} examines retrieval-augmented generation to formally prove why soft grounding falls short of structural faithfulness, while Section~\ref{sec:sbert} justifies the sentence transformer embeddings used as the semantic relevance scorer at the heart of DCA-Trie. Finally, Sections~\ref{sec:related} and~\ref{sec:synthesis} position this work against specific prior art and synthesize three unresolved research gaps that this project directly answers.

\section{Hallucination in Large Language Models: Taxonomy, Structural Causes,
  and the Scaling Paradox}
\label{sec:hallucination}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

This section explores the root cause of the problem DCA-Trie sets out to solve. Rather than treating hallucination as a simple bug that can be patched, we examine it as a fundamental consequence of how autoregressive language models operate, meaning its elimination requires direct external constraints. To conclude, the section addresses a common counterargument, explaining why simply scaling up to larger models does not make these constraint mechanisms unnecessary.

\subsection{Defining Hallucination: A Formal Taxonomy for Knowledge-Intensive
    Generation}

\citet{ji2023survey} define hallucination in natural language generation as the
production of content that is fluent and internally coherent yet factually
unsupported by, or in outright contradiction with, any verifiable source.
\citet{huang2023survey} refine this definition into a two-part taxonomy that
is foundational to the present work. \textit{Intrinsic hallucination} occurs
when the model output directly contradicts material that is explicitly present in
the provided source context. \textit{Extrinsic hallucination} occurs when the
output contains claims that cannot be verified against any provided or
accessible external source, even if those claims are not outright contradictions.

When applied to the specific domain of knowledge graph question answering (KGQA), this two-part taxonomy translates into highly concrete, operational definitions. Intrinsic hallucination in KG-constrained generation means generating a triplet $(e, r, e')$ that does not exist as a verified fact in the knowledge graph $\mathcal{G}$. Extrinsic hallucination means generating a multi-hop reasoning chain that cannot be traced to any verified path in $\mathcal{G}$, regardless of whether each individual generated token seems plausible in isolation. Both forms are unacceptable for safety-critical or knowledge-intensive applications, where factual reliability is a hard requirement rather than a desirable property. The severity of the problem is underscored by the observation in \citet{luo2024gcr} that even leading knowledge-graph-enhanced reasoning methods, such as the RoG framework, still produce hallucinated reasoning steps in approximately 33\% of cases. This single finding constitutes the strongest empirical justification for hard constraint mechanisms over soft grounding approaches.

\subsection{The Autoregressive Generation Process as the Structural Root of Hallucination}

Crucially, hallucination should not be viewed as a mere technical anomaly or a transient issue that can be easily trained away; rather, it is a fundamental architectural consequence of the autoregressive generation mechanism itself. At each generation step $t$, an autoregressive LLM computes a probability distribution over the full vocabulary conditioned on
the input prompt $x$ and all previously generated tokens $y_{<t} = (y_1, \ldots,
    y_{t-1})$:
\begin{equation}
    P(y_t \mid y_{<t},\, x)
    \label{eq:autoregressive}
\end{equation}

Three structural observations follow directly from this formulation \citep{brown2020gpt3, huang2023survey, ji2023survey}. First, no term in Equation~\eqref{eq:autoregressive} encodes a factual verification check: the distribution is computed entirely from learned weights and the conditioning context, with no mechanism for querying an external ground-truth source. Second,
because $y_{<t}$ conditions all future steps, an error introduced at step $t-1$
propagates forward as part of the conditioning context for every subsequent
token, producing grammatically fluent continuations whose factual incorrectness
has no surface signal distinguishable from a correct chain. Third, the
distribution is defined over the full vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$; every token has
strictly positive probability at every step. The consequence is that structural
hallucination is not a low-probability edge case; it is an ever-present
possibility at every decoding step, and the fluency of the generated text
provides no diagnostic signal that an error has occurred.

\subsection{The Scaling Paradox: Why Larger Models Do Not Solve Hallucination}

A natural and frequently encountered objection to constraint-based approaches is that sufficiently large models, or those trained with reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), should be factually reliable enough to render hard constraints unnecessary. The evidence does not support this objection, and establishing this point is essential for a principled motivation of DCA-Trie. \citet{lin2022truthfulqa} introduce TruthfulQA, a benchmark designed to measure whether models generate truthful answers by targeting questions that elicit common human misconceptions. Their central finding is that larger models do not systematically outperform smaller models on truthfulness and that, for certain question categories, truthfulness accuracy actually decreases with scale, a phenomenon termed \textit{inverse scaling}. This occurs because larger models natively become better at memorising and reproducing the false but highly prevalent misconceptions present in their training data.

As illustrated schematically in Figure~\ref{fig:scaling}, Truthfulness accuracy explicitly exhibits inverse scaling across base language models such as the GPT-3 and GPT-Neo families. Unconstrained language modeling performance fails to improve monotonically with parameter count and instead regresses robustly into the inverse-scaling region. By contrast, models explicitly fine-tuned on QA tasks (such as UnifiedQA) exhibit positive scaling, demonstrating that scaling standard autoregressive imitation alone inherently penalizes factual reasoning.

The sycophancy problem identified by \citet{perez2022sycophancy} compounds this picture. RLHF-tuned models are trained to produce outputs aligned with user preferences; since users tend to rate confident and fluent responses favorably, larger RLHF-tuned models are more likely to generate confident-sounding but factually incorrect statements that match expected answers rather than true ones. Confidence and accuracy are thus decoupled by the very training procedure that was supposed to improve model reliability. \citet{kandpal2023large} identify a further limitation through the memorisation-generalisation tension: larger models memorize more training data and therefore perform better on factual recall tasks involving frequently seen entity combinations, but they do not improve on compositional multi-hop questions that require systematic reasoning over entity combinations unseen during training. that require systematic reasoning over entity combinations unseen during training. Knowledge graph question answering is precisely this kind of task: the correct answer to a novel three-hop question requires chaining entities and relations in a configuration that almost certainly did not appear as a single training example. \citet{mallen2023not} corroborate this by showing that even GPT-4 hallucinates at a non-trivial rate on questions involving tail entities with low training-set frequency, regardless of the model's overall capability.

These findings collectively demonstrate that hallucination in large language models is not a limitation of scale, but a fundamental structural flaw. Autoregressive generation inherently lacks a mechanism to ground generated tokens in verifiable external sources, an absence that cannot be resolved simply by increasing parameter counts. The following sections review approaches that attempt to address this limitation, progressing from soft grounding via retrieval augmentation to the hard constraint mechanisms that motivate this dissertation.

\section{Chain-of-Thought Reasoning, Self-Consistency, and the Faithfulness
  Ceiling of Unconstrained Multi-Step Generation}
\label{sec:cot}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

A natural question often arises when evaluating large language models: if chain-of-thought prompting already yields such significant improvements in multi-step reasoning, why do we need constrained decoding at all? The answer developed in this section is that while chain-of-thought and its extensions improve reasoning \textit{accuracy in expectation}, they cannot offer a \textit{structural faithfulness guarantee}, the exact property that DCA-Trie is designed to provide. Recognizing this distinction is essential, as it highlights why prompting alone is insufficient and why constrained decoding remains fundamentally necessary.

\subsection{Chain-of-Thought Prompting: Eliciting Step-by-Step Reasoning from LLMs}

Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting, introduced by \citet{wei2022cot}, is the dominant paradigm for improving multi-step reasoning in large language models. The mechanism is straightforward: by including worked examples in the prompt that show intermediate reasoning steps leading to the final answer, the model learns to produce its own intermediate steps before giving a final answer to a new
question. This shifts the model from direct answer prediction to the decomposition of complex problems into a chain of simpler sub-problems, each resolved in sequence. \citet{wei2022cot} show that this ability is emergent: it is negligible in models below approximately 100 billion parameters and arises
sharply beyond this threshold. Empirically, CoT prompting yields substantial improvements on mathematical reasoning benchmarks such as GSM8K and MATH, as well as on multi-hop question answering tasks, in comparison with direct prompting baselines.

Formally, a CoT output for a given question $x$ is structured as a sequence of intermediate reasoning steps followed by a final answer:

\begin{equation}
    \bigl(r_1,\, r_2,\, \ldots,\, r_k,\, a\bigr)
    \label{eq:cot}
\end{equation}

where $r_i$ denotes each intermediate reasoning step and $a$ is the final answer. The language model generates each step autoregressively, using the same probabilistic mechanism described in Equation~\eqref{eq:autoregressive}:
\[
    P(r_i \mid r_{<i},\, x)
\]
The intrinsic limitation of this approach is immediately apparent: there is no mechanism to ensure that any given step $r_i$ corresponds to an actual, verified triplet within the knowledge graph $\mathcal{G}$. If the model hallucinates an intermediate step $r_i$, that error becomes part of the conditioning context for the next step $r_{i+1}$, cascading forward and irreparably compromising the final answer $a$. As a result, a CoT sequence can read as entirely fluent, coherent, and plausible while still containing one or more steps that lack any basis in verified facts.

\subsection{Self-Consistency and Tree-of-Thought: Improving Reliability Without
    Improving Faithfulness}

Two major extensions of chain-of-thought attempt to address these reliability limitations, yet neither fundamentally resolves the structural faithfulness problem. The first, self-consistency \citep{wang2023selfconsistency}, combats the high variability of individual CoT chains by sampling multiple independent reasoning paths and selecting the most frequent final answer (majority voting). This approach effectively marginalizes out anomalous reasoning errors and substantially boosts accuracy on benchmarks like GSM8K and multi-hop QA. However, it still falls short of providing a hard guarantee: there is no assurance that any single sampled chain is fully grounded in verified knowledge graph facts. It is entirely possible — especially for deep, multi-hop questions where error probabilities compound — for a majority of independently generated chains to all suffer from hallucinations, resulting in an incorrect majority-vote answer.

The second major extension, Tree-of-Thought \citep{yao2023tree}, takes a more structured approach by framing the generation process as a search over a tree of partial reasoning steps. It uses the language model as both the generator of new steps and the evaluator that scores and prunes unpromising branches. This deliberate, planning-oriented approach significantly improves performance on complex tasks that require backtracking. Ultimately, however, Tree-of-Thought operates entirely within the model's own parametric knowledge: the node scoring and pruning decisions are made by the same system generating the steps, without ever querying an external, verified knowledge graph.

The decisive observation for both of these prompting-based methods is that they can only reduce the \textit{statistical probability} of hallucinated reasoning; they cannot actually guarantee a zero-hallucination output. This is because neither method alters the fundamental token-level generation mechanism. At any given step, the valid token set remains the entire vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$, and every token retains a strictly positive probability of being selected. This highlights the critical distinction introduced by constrained decoding, which enforces absolute structural faithfulness by setting the probability of every invalid token to exactly zero.

\subsection{The Faithfulness Ceiling: The Mathematical Limits of Prompting}

The fundamental inadequacy of prompting arises directly from its probabilistic formulation. All prompting strategies, whether relying on few-shot examples, chain-of-thought decomposition, self-consistency aggregation, or tree-structured search, operate strictly by modifying the \textit{input} provided to the language model. While this favorably shifts the probability distribution toward correct sequences, it leaves the underlying autoregressive generation mechanism entirely untouched. Because decoding still occurs over the unrestricted vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$, every token technically retains a strictly positive probability of being sampled at any given step.

True structural faithfulness, defined as the absolute guarantee that every generated token forms part of a verified knowledge graph path, requires that all invalid tokens be assigned a probability of \textit{exactly} zero. This cannot be accomplished at the prompt level; it necessitates a fundamental intervention in the decoding algorithm itself, which is precisely the function of constrained decoding \citep{geng2023gcd, willard2023outlines}.

As formally distinguished by \citet{geng2023gcd} and \citet{willard2023outlines}, prompting acts as a \textit{soft influence}, whereas constrained decoding operates as a \textit{hard mechanism}. While intelligent prompting can significantly elevate factual accuracy in expectation, only constrained decoding can guarantee structural faithfulness with a probability of one.

\section{Knowledge Graph Question Answering: Structured Reasoning, Benchmark
  Design, and the Exponential Complexity of Multi-Hop Inference}
\label{sec:kgqa}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

This section establishes the technical domain in which the DCA-Trie framework operates. The principles and structural constraints introduced here provide the foundational rationale for the architectural design decisions detailed in Chapter 3.

\subsection{Knowledge Graphs as Structured Repositories of Verified Facts:
    Formal Definition and Properties}

A knowledge graph is formally defined as a directed labelled graph
$\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{E},\, \mathcal{R},\, \mathcal{T})$, where
$\mathcal{E}$ is the set of entities, $\mathcal{R}$ is the set of relation
types, and $\mathcal{T} \subseteq \mathcal{E} \times \mathcal{R} \times
    \mathcal{E}$ is the set of verified factual triplets. A standard concrete
example from Freebase \citep{bollacker2008freebase} is the triplet
$(\textit{Marie Curie},\, \textit{award\_received},\,
    \textit{Nobel\_Prize\_in\_Physics})$, which logically connects two entities via a directed relation.

Freebase encodes tens of millions of such triplets spanning geography, history,
entertainment, and science. It serves as the underlying factual resource for
both benchmark datasets evaluated in this work: WebQSP \citep{yih2016value} and
ComplexWebQuestions \citep{talmor2018web}. Given an initial entity $e_0$, a multi-hop reasoning path of length $L$ within $\mathcal{G}$ is represented as the sequence:

\begin{equation}
    p = (e_0,\, r_1,\, e_1,\, r_2,\, e_2,\, \ldots,\, r_L,\, e_L)
    \label{eq:kgpath}
\end{equation}

where each consecutive step $(e_{i-1},\, r_i,\, e_i)$ forms a valid triplet within $\mathcal{T}$. In the context of KGQA, the correct answer to a multi-hop question $q$ corresponds to the terminal entity $e_L$ of a specific, structurally sound path $p$. This formalization is load-bearing throughout this dissertation: the structural faithfulness guarantee encoded by DCA-Trie strictly mandates that every generated sequence inherently forms a valid prefix of some defined path $p$ within $\mathcal{G}$.

The critical distinction between a structured KG and an unstructured text corpus is that every triplet in $\mathcal{T}$ is human-verified, machine-readable, and unambiguously delineated. This innate topology enables KG-constrained generation to operate in a manner entirely distinct from standard retrieval augmentation (RAG). Rather than relying on a probabilistic semantic similarity search against raw text, a constraint oracle can systematically verify any candidate token against $\mathcal{G}$ via precise, deterministic graph lookups.

\subsubsection{Concrete Example: A Knowledge Graph Subgraph from Freebase}

To make the formal definition concrete, consider a small subgraph extracted from Freebase. Figure~\ref{fig:kg_example} illustrates a directed graph with six entity nodes connected by five labelled relation edges. Each edge represents a
single verified triplet $(e, r, e') \in \mathcal{T}$. For instance, the triplet $(\textit{Blue\_Hawaii},\, \textit{film.film.featured\_film\_location},\,\textit{Hawaii})$ encodes the fact that the film ``Blue Hawaii'' was filmed
in Hawaii. A valid multi-hop reasoning path through this subgraph might be:
\[
    (\textit{Blue\_Hawaii},\, \textit{film.film.featured\_film\_location},\,
    \textit{Hawaii},\, \textit{location.location.adjoining\_water},\,
    \textit{Pacific\_Ocean})
\]
This path has length $L = 2$ (two hops) and terminates at the entity \textit{Pacific\_Ocean}. If a question asks ``What large body of water adjoins the location where Blue Hawaii was filmed?'', the correct answer corresponds to
the terminal entity of this verified path. The structural faithfulness guarantee of DCA-Trie ensures that every intermediate step of the model's generated reasoning chain corresponds to an actual verified triplet in $\mathcal{T}$
making hallucinated reasoning steps mathematically impossible.

\subsection{WebQSP and ComplexWebQuestions: Benchmark Architecture, Evaluation Protocol, and Hop-Depth Stratification}

Two benchmark datasets provide the empirical evaluation framework for this
dissertation. WebQSP \citep{yih2016value} is derived from the WebQuestions
dataset and comprises 4,737 questions grounded in Freebase,
with reasoning depths of one to two hops. Each question is annotated
with its corresponding SPARQL query against Freebase and is typically evaluated using Hits@1 and F1 scores. Within the context of constrained generation, WebQSP
represents a low-complexity verification regime; because the valid path set branching from any question entity is strictly bounded, the target path is relatively straightforward to discriminate from incorrect alternatives.

ComplexWebQuestions (CWQ; \citealt{talmor2018web}) extends this paradigm by applying systematic transformations -- specifically composition, conjunction, and comparative or superlative operations -- to WebQSP seed queries. This yields
approximately 34,000 questions requiring deeper reasoning chains spanning up to four hops. For generative frameworks, CWQ
represents the combinatorial frontier where the \textit{permissiveness problem} is most acute. At a depth of four hops, the set of structurally valid paths reachable from the question entities
grows exponentially. Forced to discriminate among thousands of formally valid but
semantically irrelevant paths relying solely on its internal parametric knowledge, the LLM is exposed to the exact hallucination failure mode
that DCA-Trie targets. To systematically measure this degradation, the hop-depth-stratified evaluation protocol from
\citet{lan2022survey} is adopted for the experiments in Chapter~4.

\subsection{Multi-Hop Reasoning Complexity: Exponential Path Growth and the
    Permissiveness Problem Quantified}

The qualitative difference between one-hop and multi-hop questions can be made
precise through a combinatorial analysis. A one-hop question requires selecting
a single correct relation from the set of outgoing edges of the question entity
$e_q$. The valid candidate set is bounded by the out-degree $d$ of $e_q$.
A multi-hop question of depth $L$ requires selecting a correct sequence of
$L$ consecutive steps, where at each step the valid set multiplies. The upper
bound on the number of valid KG paths of length $L$ reachable from the question
entities $\mathcal{E}_q$ is:

\begin{equation}
    |\mathcal{P}_L| \;\leq\; |\mathcal{E}_q| \times d^L
    \label{eq:pathgrowth}
\end{equation}

where $d$ represents the average out-degree of entities in $\mathcal{G}$. Assuming a conservative average out-degree $d \approx 20$ for well-connected entities in Freebase, and a typical query linking to $|\mathcal{E}_q| \in \{1, 2, 3\}$ seed entities, we can instantiate
Equation~\eqref{eq:pathgrowth}: at $L = 2$, the upper bound is
$3 \times 400 = 1{,}200$ valid paths; at $L = 3$, the bound grows to
$3 \times 8{,}000 = 24{,}000$ valid paths; at $L = 4$, the bound reaches
$3 \times 160{,}000 = 480{,}000$ valid paths. In each case, exactly one path is the correct targeted answer.

This exponential growth formally quantifies the permissiveness problem inherent to GCR
and DoG. When the constraint oracle relies purely on structural validity, $f(\mathcal{G}, \mathcal{E}_q)$, the
valid token set at every generation step is flooded with thousands of structurally
permitted but semantically irrelevant paths. The LLM is consequently forced to discriminate among
them relying solely on its internal parametric knowledge -- precisely the hallucination-prone
failure mode that constrained decoding was originally designed to suppress
\citep{luo2024gcr, li2024dog}. The Semantic Irrelevance Ratio (SIR), introduced
in Chapter~3 of this dissertation, provides the first direct measure of this
phenomenon. Empirical evidence for performance degradation as a function of hop

\section{Constrained Decoding for Faithful Text Generation: From Logit Masking
  to Knowledge Graph Oracles}
\label{sec:constrained}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

This section establishes the technical lineage of constrained decoding paradigms leading to the proposed framework. It progresses from the foundational logit-masking mechanism through grammar-based constraints to contemporary knowledge-graph-specific oracles. Each framework reviewed serves either as a direct methodological baseline or as a foundational model whose specific structural limitations the DCA-Trie architecture is designed to resolve.

\subsection{The Logit-Masking Paradigm: Hard Constraints on Autoregressive
    Vocabulary Selection}

The logit-masking paradigm is the foundational mechanism for hard-constrained
generation \citep{geng2023gcd, willard2023outlines}. The key insight is that
rather than influencing the probability distribution over tokens, as prompting
does, a constraint oracle computes a binary mask that sets the logit of every
invalid token to negative infinity (effectively zero probability after softmax)
before the token selection step. This renders the generation of invalid tokens
mathematically impossible rather than merely improbable.

Formally, let $\boldsymbol{\alpha}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{V}|}$ be the raw
logit vector at decoding step $t$, and let $\mathbf{m}_t \in \{0, 1\}^{|\mathcal{V}|}$
be the binary validity mask computed by the constraint oracle. The masked logit
vector is:

\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}_t \;=\; \mathbf{m}_t \odot \boldsymbol{\alpha}_t
    \label{eq:logitmask}
\end{equation}

where $\odot$ denotes element-wise product (with the convention that
$0 \odot \alpha = -\infty$ before softmax). Token selection under greedy
decoding is $y_t = \argmax(\tilde{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}_t)$. Under beam search
with beam width $k$, the top-$k$ partial sequences are maintained and ranked by
cumulative log-probability of masked logits.

The central research question of this dissertation follows directly from this
formulation: \textit{how should $\mathbf{m}_t$ be computed?} The answer
determines the expressiveness, faithfulness, and efficiency of the entire
framework. GCR computes $\mathbf{m}_t$ from a function $f(\mathcal{G},
    \mathcal{E}_q)$ evaluated once before generation. DCA-Trie computes
$\mathbf{m}_t$ from a function $f(\mathcal{G}, \mathcal{E}_q, q, y_{<t})$
updated at every step. Every subsequent subsection is a step in the progression
toward this answer.

\subsection{Grammar-Constrained Decoding: GCD and Outlines as Foundational
    Format Constraints}

\citet{geng2023gcd} introduce Grammar-Constrained Decoding (GCD), which
constrains generation to strings conforming to a context-free grammar by
using an Earley parser as the constraint oracle. \citet{willard2023outlines}
introduce Outlines, which achieves the same goal for regular-expression and
JSON-schema constraints via finite-state automata. Both works establish the
critical efficiency insight that the constraint structure can be precomputed
offline, as parse states or automaton transitions, and queried in amortised
constant time at each inference step. This precomputation insight is central to
DCA-Trie v1, which preserves it through static semantic filtering at trie
construction time.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{algorithm}[H]
        \caption{Grammar-Constrained Decoding Algorithm}
        \begin{algorithmic}[1]
            \Require Context-free grammar $\mathcal{G}_{\text{CFG}}$; LLM with vocabulary
            $\mathcal{V}$; prompt $x$
            \Ensure Output sequence $y_1 \ldots y_T$ valid under $\mathcal{G}_{\text{CFG}}$
            \State \textbf{Precompute:} Earley parse automaton $\mathcal{A}$ from
            $\mathcal{G}_{\text{CFG}}$
            \For{$t = 1$ \textbf{to} $T$}
            \State $\mathcal{V}_t \gets \{v \in \mathcal{V} : y_{<t} \| v \text{ is a valid
                    grammar prefix under } \mathcal{A}\}$
            \Comment{Oracle query}
            \State $\mathbf{m}_t[v] \gets \mathbf{1}[v \in \mathcal{V}_t]$ for all
            $v \in \mathcal{V}$
            \State $\tilde{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}_t \gets \mathbf{m}_t \odot
                \textsc{LLMLogits}(y_{<t}, x)$
            \State $y_t \gets \argmax(\tilde{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}_t)$
            \EndFor
            \State \Return $y_1 \ldots y_T$
        \end{algorithmic}
    \end{algorithm}
    \caption{Grammar-Constrained Decoding Algorithm.}
    \label{alg:gcd}
\end{figure}

The shared limitation of GCD and Outlines is stated precisely: both frameworks
constrain the \textit{format} of the output (grammar, schema, structure) but not
the \textit{factual content}. A grammatically valid JSON response can contain
entirely hallucinated facts. As annotated in Figure~\ref{alg:gcd}, the valid
set $\mathcal{V}_t$ depends only on the partial string and the grammar; there is
no dependence on question semantics or on a verified factual source. This is the
same structural limitation present in GCR's KG-Trie, which constrains to KG
path format while remaining semantically blind to the question. The transition
from format constraints to knowledge-graph content constraints is the next step
in the progression.

\subsection{Graph-Constrained Reasoning (GCR): Structural Faithfulness via
    KG-Trie Oracles}

Graph-Constrained Reasoning (GCR; \citealt{luo2024gcr}) is the primary baseline
for this dissertation and represents the state of the art in KG-constrained
decoding at the time of this work. GCR introduces five components. First,
\textit{entity linking} identifies the question entity set $\mathcal{E}_q$ from
the natural language question before generation begins. Second,
\textit{KG-Trie construction} performs a breadth-first search from
$\mathcal{E}_q$ up to $L$ hops, encoding all reachable KG paths as a prefix
tree. A trie node at depth $d$ corresponds to a partial path of $d$ tokens.
Third, \textit{constrained decoding} queries the trie at each generation step
with the current partial generation $y_{<t}$ to return the set of tokens that
extend a valid trie prefix. Fourth, the \textit{faithfulness guarantee} follows:
because every token must correspond to a valid trie prefix, and every trie path
corresponds to a verified triplet chain in $\mathcal{T}$, structural hallucination
is mathematically impossible. Fifth, GCR achieves 92.6 Hits@1 on WebQSP and
75.8 Hits@1 on CWQ with verified 100\% structural faithfulness using a
Llama-3.1-8B backbone \citep{luo2024gcr}.

Formally, the valid token set under GCR is:

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{W}_{\text{val}}^{\text{GCR}} \;=\; f(\mathcal{G},\, \mathcal{E}_q)
    \label{eq:gcr}
\end{equation}

The KG-Trie $\mathcal{T}_{\text{trie}}$ is constructed as
$\mathcal{T}_{\text{trie}} = \textsc{BFS}(\mathcal{G}, \mathcal{E}_q, L)$.
At each step $t$:
\[
    \mathcal{V}_t = \bigl\{v \in \mathcal{V} : \text{prefix}(y_{<t}) \| v \in
    \mathcal{T}_{\text{trie}}\bigr\}
\]

The critical annotation is that $\mathcal{W}_{\text{val}}^{\text{GCR}}$ has
\textit{no dependence} on $q$ (the question text) or on $y_{<t}$ (the partial
generation state). The valid set is semantically blind and computationally fixed
before generation begins. This is the exact formulation that DCA-Trie upgrades
to $\mathcal{W}_{\text{val}}^{\text{DCA}}(t) = f(\mathcal{G}, \mathcal{E}_q, q,
    y_{<t})$.

\subsection{Decoding on Graphs (DoG): Step-Wise Topological Expansion and Its
    Persistent Limitations}

\citet{li2024dog} introduce Decoding on Graphs (DoG) as a partial remedy to
GCR's static trie. DoG introduces step-wise trie expansion: after committing to
an entity $e_t$ during generation, the valid set is extended with all KG
neighbors of $e_t$. Formally, after committing entity $e_t$:

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{T}_{t+1} \;=\; \mathcal{T}_t \;\cup\; \bigl\{(e_t,\, r,\, e') :
    (e_t,\, r,\, e') \in \mathcal{T}\text{ for all } r, e'\bigr\}
    \label{eq:dog}
\end{equation}

This makes the constraint topologically dynamic: it grows as reasoning
progresses through the graph. DoG offers two genuine improvements over GCR.
First, the valid set at step $t$ reflects the entities already committed to in
the reasoning chain, so the trie cannot contain paths that are structurally
inconsistent with the committed reasoning direction. Second, the dynamic
expansion can handle knowledge graphs too large to enumerate statically within
the pre-generation BFS.

However, DoG has two persistent limitations. First, as is explicit in
Equation~\eqref{eq:dog}, the expansion rule is semantically blind: every
neighbor of every visited entity is admitted to $\mathcal{T}_{t+1}$ regardless
of its relevance to $q$ or to the partial generation $y_{<t}$. The permissiveness
problem therefore persists: the number of paths in the valid set may remain in
the thousands at every step. Second, DoG sacrifices the computational efficiency
of precomputed trie structures identified by \citet{willard2023outlines}: because
the trie must be rebuilt at each step, the amortised per-step query cost of
precomputed structures is forfeited.

DCA-Trie addresses both limitations simultaneously. The DCA-Trie v2 expansion
rule replaces the semantically blind expansion of Equation~\eqref{eq:dog} with a
semantically conditioned rule (defined formally in Chapter~3):
\[
    \mathcal{T}_{t+1} = \mathcal{T}_t \cup \bigl\{(e_t,\, r,\, e') :
    \textsc{score}(e',\, q,\, y_{<t}) \geq \tau\bigr\}
\]
The single structural change, conditioning expansion on a semantic relevance
score, is the core algorithmic contribution of DCA-Trie v2.

\section{Beam Search Decoding and Prefix-Tree Constraint Integration: Mechanics,
  Efficiency, and the Step-Wise Constraint Problem}
\label{sec:beam}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

DCA-Trie v2 operates step-wise during beam search. Reviewers evaluating the
method chapter need to understand both the beam search mechanism and how
prefix-tree constraints interact with beam expansion before the DCA-Trie design
can be followed. This section provides that foundation.

\subsection{Beam Search: Approximate Global Optimization Over Autoregressive
    Sequences}

Beam search is the standard approximate decoding algorithm for autoregressive
sequence generation \citep{holtzman2020curious}. The algorithm maintains a
\textit{beam} (a set of $k$ partial sequences) at each generation step. At
step $t$, every beam hypothesis is expanded by every vocabulary token, masked
logit scores are computed, and only the top-$k$ hypotheses by cumulative
log-probability are retained. Formally, for each beam hypothesis
$h \in \mathcal{B}_t$:

\begin{equation}
    \textsc{score}(h \| v) = \textsc{score}(h) +
    \log \textsc{softmax}\bigl(\tilde{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}_t\bigr)[v],
    \quad \forall\, v \in \mathcal{V} \text{ with } \tilde{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}_t[v] > 0
    \label{eq:beamsearch}
\end{equation}

The updated beam is $\mathcal{B}_{t+1} = \text{top-}k$ over all $(h, v)$
pairs by $\textsc{score}(h \| v)$. The beam width $k$ controls the
exploration-exploitation tradeoff: $k = 1$ reduces to greedy decoding; larger
$k$ explores a wider portion of the generation space at the cost of $k$-fold
compute per step.

When combined with the logit mask from Equation~\eqref{eq:logitmask}, beam
search operates exclusively within the valid subspace defined by the constraint
oracle. The KG-Trie defines this subspace structurally; DCA-Trie's semantic
filter narrows it further, reducing the number of $(h, v)$ candidate pairs that
must be scored at each beam expansion step. This reduction is what makes
DCA-Trie computationally competitive with GCR despite the additional scoring
overhead.

\subsection{Prefix Trees as Constraint Oracles: Efficiency, Precomputation, and
    the Dynamic Rebuilding Cost}

A prefix tree (trie; \citealt{fredkin1960trie}) over KG paths supports $O(d)$
lookup at each generation step: given the current partial sequence $y_{<t}$, the
set of valid next tokens is exactly the set of children of the current trie node.
This is the efficiency insight of \citet{willard2023outlines}: precomputing the
trie amortises the constraint lookup cost across all generation steps, making
constrained decoding practical at inference time. The trie construction
complexity is $O(|\mathcal{P}| \times L)$, where $|\mathcal{P}|$ is the number
of valid paths encoded and $L$ is the maximum path length.

DoG's step-wise expansion sacrifices this benefit by rebuilding the trie at each
step at a cost of $O(|\mathcal{N}_t| \times d)$ per step, where
$|\mathcal{N}_t|$ is the number of newly reachable entities at step $t$. While
not prohibitive per step, this cost accumulates over $L$ steps and eliminates
the precomputation advantage entirely. DCA-Trie v1 (static semantic filtering at
construction time) preserves the precomputation benefit entirely, since the
filtered trie is built once before generation. DCA-Trie v2 (dynamic step-wise
expansion with semantic filtering) incurs per-step rebuilding overhead similar
to DoG, but the semantic filter prunes most candidate expansions, so the
effective expansion set $|\mathcal{N}_t^{\text{filtered}}|$ is substantially
smaller than $|\mathcal{N}_t|$ in DoG.

    \caption{Interaction between prefix-tree constraint lookup and beam search expansion.}
    \label{fig:beamtrie}
\end{figure}


\subsection{The Step-Wise Constraint Problem: When the Constraint Must Know What
    Has Been Generated}

The step-wise constraint problem is the core design challenge motivating
DCA-Trie. In GCR, the constraint oracle is computed once before generation and
queried at each step without memory of what has been generated beyond the
structural trie path. In DCA-Trie v2, the oracle is updated after each
committed entity to reflect the semantic direction of the current reasoning
chain. This requires the oracle to maintain state across generation steps;
specifically, the partial generation $y_{<t}$ and the question embedding must
be re-incorporated at each update.

The formal requirement is:

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{W}_{\text{val}}^{\text{DCA}}(t) \;=\;
    f\!\bigl(\mathcal{G},\, \mathcal{E}_q,\, q,\, y_{<t}\bigr)
    \label{eq:dcareq}
\end{equation}

where the function $f$ must satisfy three conditions. (i) \textit{Structural
    faithfulness}: every token $v \in \mathcal{W}_{\text{val}}^{\text{DCA}}(t)$
corresponds to a valid KG path prefix in $\mathcal{G}$. (ii)
\textit{Semantic relevance}: the proportion of $\mathcal{W}_{\text{val}}^{\text{DCA}}(t)$
consisting of semantically irrelevant paths is strictly less than that of
$\mathcal{W}_{\text{val}}^{\text{GCR}}$. (iii) \textit{Recall preservation}:
the gold answer path is never excluded from $\mathcal{W}_{\text{val}}^{\text{DCA}}(t)$,
with false negative rate held below 5\% on the WebQSP validation set. These
three conditions constitute the formal specification of DCA-Trie, evaluated
quantitatively in Chapter~4.

\section{Retrieval-Augmented Generation: Architectural Variants, KG-Augmented
  Approaches, and the Hard Limit of Soft Grounding}
\label{sec:rag}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Retrieval-augmented generation is the dominant paradigm for grounding LLM
outputs in external knowledge. This section reviews the foundational RAG
architecture, KG-specific retrieval variants, and the latest advanced RAG
systems, before providing a formal impossibility argument establishing that no
RAG variant can achieve the structural faithfulness guarantees that constrained
decoding provides.

\subsection{The RAG Paradigm: Dense Retrieval, Fusion-in-Decoder, and the Soft
    Grounding Ceiling}

\citet{lewis2020retrieval} introduce Retrieval-Augmented Generation, in which a
dense retriever selects relevant passages from an external knowledge source based
on embedding similarity, and a reader LLM generates the answer conditioned on
both the question and the retrieved passages. The retriever is typically
implemented as a dual-encoder (Dense Passage Retrieval; \citealt{karpukhin2020dpr}),
computing embedding similarity between the question and candidate passages
offline. Fusion-in-Decoder \citep{izacard2021leveraging} extends the original
RAG architecture by encoding all retrieved passages independently through the
encoder and fusing their representations in the decoder, improving coverage
across a larger set of retrieved documents.

The critical shared limitation of these approaches is architectural: retrieved
context is a \textit{soft influence}: the LLM is free to generate any token
regardless of the retrieved material. The retriever controls what the LLM
\textit{sees}; the constraint oracle controls what the LLM may
\textit{generate}. RAG addresses the former; constrained decoding addresses the
latter. \citet{agrawal2024mindful} document the concrete consequence: even with
explicit KG access, RAG-augmented models produce hallucinated reasoning steps in
a substantial fraction of cases.

\subsection{KG-Augmented Retrieval: Structured Context Without Structural
    Guarantees}

Several works augment the RAG paradigm with KG subgraphs rather than text
passages, bringing the approach closer in spirit to constrained KG decoding.
\citet{he2021multihop} use attention over retrieved KG subgraphs to guide
multi-hop reasoning chains. \citet{yasunaga2021qagnn} combine text and KG
context via graph neural networks, allowing the model to attend jointly to
retrieved passages and KG neighbourhoods. \citet{shi2021kgpath} retrieve KG
paths as natural language context strings.

For each of these approaches, the faithfulness limitation is identical: the
retrieved KG subgraph or path is provided to the LLM as additional input
context. The LLM may attend to it, partially ignore it, or generate claims that
go beyond it. The KG is advisory, not constraining. This is the decisive
architectural distinction from GCR and DCA-Trie, where the KG is
\textit{mechanically} enforced through the logit mask rather than provided as
contextual suggestion.

\subsection{Advanced RAG Variants: Self-RAG, FLARE, IRCoT, and GraphRAG}

Four advanced RAG variants warrant individual analysis as potential alternatives
to constrained decoding that a reviewer may raise.

Self-RAG \citep{asai2024selfrag} augments the standard RAG pipeline by inserting
special reflection tokens that allow the model to decide dynamically when
retrieval is needed and whether retrieved passages are relevant or supportive.
This improves retrieval precision and citation accuracy. However, Self-RAG does
not modify the token selection mechanism: the LLM remains free to generate any
token at any step, and the reflection tokens are themselves generated
autoregressively. Unlike DCA-Trie, Self-RAG cannot guarantee structural
faithfulness.

FLARE \citep{jiang2023flare} uses the LLM's next-token prediction probability as
a signal to trigger retrieval when confidence falls below a threshold. This
addresses the question of \textit{when} to retrieve but leaves the generation
faithfulness problem untouched: retrieved context is again a soft input
influence.

IRCoT \citep{trivedi2022ircot} interleaves retrieval with chain-of-thought
reasoning steps, performing a retrieval call at each CoT step. This substantially
improves multi-hop accuracy by ensuring that each reasoning step is informed by
freshly retrieved context. Each CoT step nevertheless remains an unconstrained
autoregressive generation, retaining strictly positive probability over all
vocabulary tokens.

GraphRAG \citep{edge2024graphrag} is the strongest RAG-based competitor to
DCA-Trie, as it explicitly retrieves community-structured graph context to
support complex relational queries. GraphRAG retrieves graph context but does
not constrain decoding: a generation conditioned on GraphRAG context can still
produce tokens that correspond to no KG triplet in $\mathcal{T}$.

For every variant, the same single sentence applies: unlike DCA-Trie, none of
these methods modifies the token selection mechanism and therefore none can
guarantee structural faithfulness.

\subsection{The Formal Impossibility of Structural Faithfulness Under Soft
    Grounding}

The following argument establishes that no retrieval-augmented approach can
provide structural faithfulness guarantees as defined in this dissertation.

\textit{Claim:} Any approach that grounds LLM generation by modifying its input
(retrieved context, subgraphs, passages) without modifying the logit computation
mechanism cannot guarantee structural faithfulness with probability one.

\textit{Proof sketch:} Structural faithfulness requires that every generated
token $y_t$ corresponds to a verified KG triplet. This requires that every
token $v \notin \mathcal{W}_{\text{val}}(t)$ has exactly zero probability at
step $t$, i.e., $P(v \mid y_{<t}, x) = 0$ for all invalid $v$. Under any RAG
approach, the LLM's raw logit distribution assigns strictly positive logit to
all tokens in $\mathcal{V}$ (or at least to all tokens not explicitly set to
$-\infty$). Retrieval augmentation modifies the input $x$ to include retrieved
context $\hat{x}$, but does not modify the logit computation
$P(\cdot \mid y_{<t}, \hat{x})$: the distribution remains over the full
vocabulary with strictly positive probabilities. Therefore, for any RAG approach,
there exists strictly positive probability of generating a non-KG-valid token at
any step. Probability-one structural faithfulness is not achievable under this
architecture. The only mechanism that achieves it is a constraint oracle that
explicitly sets $\alpha_t[v] = -\infty$ for all invalid $v$: the logit-masking
mechanism of Equation~\eqref{eq:logitmask}, as implemented in GCR, DoG, and
DCA-Trie. \citet{luo2024gcr} provide empirical confirmation: GCR achieves 100\%
structural faithfulness where RAG-based approaches do not. \hfill$\square$

\section{Semantic Relevance Scoring with Sentence Transformer Embeddings:
  Training Objectives, Architecture Selection, and Application to Constraint
  Oracle Construction}
\label{sec:sbert}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

This section justifies every design choice in DCA-Trie's semantic relevance
scorer, responding in advance to the reviewer objections: ``Why not BM25?'',
``Why not use the LLM's own hidden states?'', and ``Why all-MiniLM-L6-v2 over a
stronger encoder?''

\subsection{Sentence-BERT: Training Objective, Architecture, and the Semantic
    Similarity Property}

\citet{reimers2019sentence} introduce Sentence-BERT (SBERT), a modification of
the BERT architecture trained specifically to produce dense vector representations
in which semantically similar sentences have high cosine similarity. The
architecture uses a BERT-like transformer encoder with mean-pooling over token
representations to produce a fixed-length sentence embedding. SBERT is trained
using a Siamese network on labelled sentence pairs. The classification-objective
training loss for a pair $(s_1, s_2)$ with embeddings $\mathbf{u} = E(s_1)$ and
$\mathbf{v} = E(s_2)$ is:

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \textsc{CrossEntropy}\Bigl(
    \text{softmax}\bigl(\mathbf{W}_t \cdot (\mathbf{u},\, \mathbf{v},\,
            |\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}|)\bigr),\; y
    \Bigr)
    \label{eq:sbert}
\end{equation}

where $(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}, |\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}|)$ is the
concatenation of the two embeddings with their element-wise absolute difference,
$\mathbf{W}_t$ is a trainable classification head, and $y \in \{\text{entailment},
    \text{neutral}, \text{contradiction}\}$. This objective pulls embeddings of
entailed pairs together and pushes contradiction pairs apart in the representation
space. At inference time, $\cos(E(s_1), E(s_2))$ measures the degree of semantic
alignment between two sentences without requiring the classification head,
enabling cosine similarity to serve as a reliable semantic relevance proxy
without any task-specific fine-tuning.

\subsection{Encoder Architecture Selection: all-MiniLM-L6-v2 vs Alternatives}

DCA-Trie requires a semantic encoder that is invoked at every beam search step
during constrained decoding. The selection of the encoder architecture therefore
involves a tradeoff between semantic quality and inference latency that is more
stringent than standard offline retrieval settings. Table~\ref{tab:encoders}
presents a systematic comparison of five candidate architectures.

\noindent \textit{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} \citep{reimers2019sentence} is a six-layer
MiniLM model distilled from a larger teacher, producing 384-dimensional
embeddings with approximately 22M parameters and roughly 80ms inference latency
on CPU. It achieves strong semantic performance on STS benchmarks while
maintaining the lowest inference latency among semantically competitive models.

\noindent \textit{all-mpnet-base-v2} achieves stronger semantic performance but
requires approximately 110M parameters and incurs around 4× the inference
latency. For iterative beam-search decoding in which the scorer is called at
every step, this latency accumulates unacceptably over the full generation
length.

\noindent \textit{BGE-M3} \citep{chen2024bge} is a state-of-the-art multilingual
retrieval encoder with stronger performance than MiniLM, but its 568M parameters
and resulting inference latency make it infeasible for step-wise decoding on a
single A100 GPU.

\noindent \textit{LLM hidden states} as a relevance scorer would be circular:
the LLM's last-layer representations encode the same parametric biases that cause
hallucination. Using them as a relevance filter does not introduce any external
grounding and defeats the purpose of the constraint mechanism.

\noindent \textit{BM25} is fast and interpretable but a lexical retrieval
method: it cannot capture semantic similarity between paraphrase-equivalent KG
paths that share the same meaning but no surface tokens, which is common in
Freebase relation strings.

The conclusion is that all-MiniLM-L6-v2 provides the optimal balance of
semantic quality and inference speed for iterative step-wise constraint
filtering.

\subsection{Cosine Similarity as a Hard Constraint Signal: From Soft Retrieval
    to Binary Admission}

In standard dense retrieval, cosine similarity produces a continuous relevance
ranking over candidate documents. DCA-Trie's key conceptual contribution in this
component is the conversion of this continuous signal into a \textit{binary
    admission criterion} that determines which tokens the LLM is permitted to
generate.

Given a candidate KG path $p$ expressed as a natural language string
$(e_0 \, r_1 \, e_1 \, r_2 \, e_2 \ldots)$, the question $q$, and the partial
generation $y_{<t}$, the semantic relevance score is:

\begin{equation}
    \textsc{score}(p,\, q,\, y_{<t}) \;=\;
    \cos\!\bigl(E(p),\;\, E(\text{concat}(q,\, y_{<t}))\bigr)
    \label{eq:score}
\end{equation}

where $E(\cdot)$ is the all-MiniLM-L6-v2 encoder. The binary admission
criterion is: admit $p$ to $\mathcal{W}_{\text{val}}^{\text{DCA}}(t)$ if and
only if $\textsc{score}(p, q, y_{<t}) \geq \tau$. This threshold function
converts the continuous cosine similarity into a binary mask entry, integrating
semantic relevance directly into the logit-masking framework of
Equation~\eqref{eq:logitmask}. The threshold $\tau$ is the key hyperparameter
controlling the precision-recall tradeoff of the relevance filter; its selection
procedure is described in Chapter~3 and evaluated in Chapter~4.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Sections~\ref{sec:hallucination}--\ref{sec:sbert} have surveyed the landscape
thematically. This section positions DCA-Trie against the specific papers a
reviewer is most likely to cite as prior art.

\subsection{KG-Constrained Decoding: GCR, DoG, and the Unresolved
    Permissiveness Problem}

GCR \citep{luo2024gcr} constructs a static KG-Trie from $f(\mathcal{G},
    \mathcal{E}_q)$ before generation, achieving 92.6 Hits@1 on WebQSP and 100\%
structural faithfulness; however, the valid token set is semantically blind and
computationally identical at every generation step (Section~\ref{sec:constrained}).
DoG \citep{li2024dog} extends GCR with step-wise topological trie expansion
after each committed entity, improving dynamic responsiveness at the cost of
per-step reconstruction; but as shown in Equation~\eqref{eq:dog}, the expansion
rule admits all KG neighbours regardless of semantic relevance, so the
permissiveness problem persists. Both works leave unresolved the central
limitation: the valid token set contains thousands of structurally correct but
contextually irrelevant paths at every generation step, forcing the LLM to rely
on hallucination-prone parametric knowledge for semantic discrimination. DCA-Trie
addresses this directly by conditioning the valid token set on the input question
semantics and the partial generation, formally upgrading the oracle from
$f(\mathcal{G}, \mathcal{E}_q)$ to $f(\mathcal{G}, \mathcal{E}_q, q, y_{<t})$
(Equation~\ref{eq:dcareq}). DCA-Trie is designed as a direct extension of GCR
and DoG, preserving their structural faithfulness guarantee while resolving the
permissiveness problem neither addresses.

\subsection{Hallucination Mitigation and KGQA: The Soft-to-Hard Spectrum}

Soft grounding approaches constitute one cluster of related work. RAG
\citep{lewis2020retrieval}, Self-RAG \citep{asai2024selfrag}, GraphRAG
\citep{edge2024graphrag}, DAGQA \citep{he2021multihop}, QA-GNN
\citep{yasunaga2021qagnn}, and self-consistency \citep{wang2023selfconsistency}
all improve faithfulness in expectation by providing the LLM with relevant
external context or by aggregating over multiple generation samples. As
established formally in Section~\ref{sec:rag}, none of these approaches modifies
the token selection mechanism: they cannot guarantee structural faithfulness with
probability one, because every token in $\mathcal{V}$ retains strictly positive
probability at every step.

Format-constrained decoding constitutes the second cluster. GCD \citep{geng2023gcd},
Outlines \citep{willard2023outlines}, and entity-constrained generation
\citep{decao2021autoregressive} establish the logit-masking paradigm on which
DCA-Trie builds. These works constrain output format or entity vocabulary rather
than multi-hop KG path faithfulness: their constraint oracle is a function of a
grammar or entity list, not of a knowledge graph with relational structure.
DCA-Trie inherits the logit-masking mechanism from this line of work and extends
it to semantically conditioned KG path constraints.

\subsection{Semantic Similarity for Graph Reasoning: The Retrieval-Constraint Boundary}

Several works apply semantic similarity signals in graph-based reasoning. Path retrieval for KGQA \citep{shi2021kgpath, saxena2022kgqa} uses semantic similarity to rank candidate paths for retrieval. Attention-guided graph traversal in DAGQA \citep{he2021multihop} uses attention weights to focus multi-hop reasoning.Semantic path reranking approaches select among candidate answers after generation. In every case, semantic similarity operates as a \textit{soft signal}  that influences which paths the LLM sees or which answer is selected after generation; it does not apply semantic similarity as a hard binary admission criterion that determines which tokens the LLM is
\textit{permitted} to generate.

This distinction between soft signals and hard constraints defines the exact novelty boundary of DCA-Trie. No prior work combines semantic relevance scoring with hard constraint oracle construction. Works that provide structural faithfulness (GCR,DoG) do not condition on semantics. Works that use semantic similarity (path retrieval, reranking) do
not provide structural faithfulness. DCA-Trie is the first framework to achieve both simultaneously, and the Semantic Irrelevance Ratio introduced in Chapter~3 is the first metric designed to measure this property directly.

\subsection{Concurrent and Subsequent Work: Positioning DCA-Trie}
\label{sec:concurrent_work}

The research landscape has evolved rapidly since the foundational works of GCR~\citep{luo2024gcr} and DoG~\citep{li2024dog}. Most notably, \textbf{RouterKGQA}~\citep{yuan2026routerkgqa} proposes a specialized--general model routing framework that achieves strong performance with minimal inference cost. RouterKGQA employs constraint-aware reasoning paths and uses SBERT (nomic-embed-text-v1) for semantic filtering during answer selection, achieving 3.57 F1 point improvements over GCR on WebQSP and CWQ while requiring only 1.15 LLM calls per question~\citep{yuan2026routerkgqa}.

\subsubsection{Differentiation of DCA-Trie}

While RouterKGQA and DCA-Trie both employ semantic relevance scoring, they operate at fundamentally different architectural levels:

RouterKGQA's semantic filtering operates on complete reasoning paths after generation, whereas DCA-Trie's semantic scoring constrains the \emph{decoding process itself}--ensuring that the LLM never sees semantically irrelevant tokens during generation. This distinction is critical: DCA-Trie reduces the cognitive load on the LLM by narrowing the valid vocabulary at each step, while RouterKGQA relies on the LLM to generate paths then filters retrospectively.

Furthermore, DCA-Trie introduces the \textbf{Semantic Irrelevance Ratio (SIR)}, the first metric to isolate and quantify constraint permissiveness independently of downstream generation accuracy. RouterKGQA does not provide a direct measure of how many structurally valid but semantically irrelevant paths enter its candidate set; DCA-Trie's SIR metric fills this evaluation gap.

  Contribution}

This section synthesizes the findings of the literature review by formalizing three distinct research gaps. Each limitation is then explicitly mapped to a proposed component of the DCA-Trie architecture and its corresponding project objective, establishing the foundation for the methodology detailed in Chapter~3.

\subsection{Three Unresolved Gaps at the Intersection of Structural Faithfulness,
    Semantic Relevance, and Constraint Measurability}

\textbf{Gap~1: The Permissiveness Problem.} GCR and DoG construct constraint oracles as $f(\mathcal{G}, \mathcal{E}_q)$, independent of question semantics and partial generation. For a three-hop CWQ question, Equation~\eqref{eq:pathgrowth} shows this produces a valid token set containing up to 24,000 structurally correct but semantically irrelevant paths, forcing the LLM to use hallucination-prone parametric knowledge for discrimination, precisely the mechanism constrained decoding was designed to suppress \citep{luo2024gcr,li2024dog}.

\textbf{Gap~2: The Static-Dynamic Efficiency Tradeoff.} GCR's precomputed
static trie is computationally efficient but semantically blind. DoG's step-wise
topological expansion introduces dynamism at the cost of per-step trie
reconstruction and without semantic filtering \citep{li2024dog,
    willard2023outlines}. No existing framework achieves semantic responsiveness
while preserving the efficiency of a precomputed oracle.

\textbf{Gap~3: The Absence of a Permissiveness Metric.} No existing work
quantifies constraint permissiveness independently of downstream answer accuracy,
making it impossible to measure progress on reducing irrelevant paths in the valid
set directly or to compare frameworks on this property \citep{luo2024gcr,
    li2024dog}.

\subsection{DCA-Trie as the Unified Response: Gap-to-Objective Mapping}

Each gap maps directly to a DCA-Trie component and project objective. \textbf{Gap~1} is addressed by DCA-Trie's semantic relevance scorer applied as a hard admission criterion, formally upgrading the constraint oracle from $f(\mathcal{G}, \mathcal{E}_q)$ to $f(\mathcal{G}, \mathcal{E}_q, q, y_{<t})$, targeting project Objectives~ii, iii, and~iv. \textbf{Gap~2} is addressed by the two-variant design: DCA-Trie v1 applies semantic filtering at trie construction time, preserving precomputation efficiency (Objective~iii); DCA-Trie v2 applies step-wise dynamic expansion with semantic filtering for maximum contextual responsiveness (Objective~iv). \textbf{Gap~3} is addressed by the Semantic Irrelevance Ratio (SIR), a new metric introduced in Chapter~3 that quantifies the proportion of the valid set consisting of semantically irrelevant paths (Objective~i).

DCA-Trie is the first constrained decoding framework to simultaneously address all three gaps, providing structural faithfulness, semantic relevance conditioning, and a dedicated permissiveness metric. RouterKGQA addresses efficiency through routing but does not provide deterministic faithfulness guarantees or constraint-level evaluation; DCA-Trie fills these critical gaps. These constitute the three novel contributions of this dissertation.
