\chapter{Literature Review}

\section{Introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) have fundamentally transformed natural language processing, demonstrating remarkable capabilities across a wide range of tasks. However, their reliance on autoregressive generation introduces a critical vulnerability: the propensity to confidently generate fluent but factually incorrect information, known as hallucination~\citep{ji2023survey}. In domains requiring precise, verifiable reasoning, such as knowledge-intensive question answering, this lack of factual reliability remains a significant barrier. To address this, the field has increasingly looked toward grounding language models in structured, external facts using knowledge graphs. Yet, ensuring that a model remains strictly faithful to these graphs during generation, without sacrificing fluency, remains an open and complex challenge.

The purpose of this chapter is to survey the existing literature that contextualizes and motivates the research problem this dissertation addresses: how to structurally guarantee factual faithfulness in multi-hop language model reasoning. Rather than describing the proposed system itself, this review maps the intersecting research landscapes of language model hallucination, multi-step reasoning, and structured knowledge grounding. By critically examining current mitigation strategies, ranging from advanced prompting to retrieval-augmented generation and early constrained decoding frameworks, this chapter establishes the fundamental limitations of approaches that rely solely on soft grounding or static constraints. Ultimately, it defines the specific boundaries where this dissertation makes its contribution.

The review is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:llms} covers the fundamentals of Large Language Models. Section~\ref{sec:hallucination} establishes hallucination in large language models as a structural problem inherent to autoregressive generation. Section~\ref{sec:cot} examines chain-of-thought reasoning and its extensions, showing why improved prompting strategies cannot provide structural faithfulness guarantees. Section~\ref{sec:kgqa} introduces knowledge graphs and knowledge graph question answering, including the benchmarks and the complexity properties that make multi-hop KGQA challenging. Section~\ref{sec:constrained} traces the development of constrained decoding from foundational logit masking through grammar constraints to knowledge graph-specific frameworks. Section~\ref{sec:rag} reviews retrieval-augmented generation and establishes why soft grounding approaches cannot achieve structural faithfulness. Section~\ref{sec:sbert} reviews semantic similarity scoring with sentence transformers. Section~\ref{sec:related} surveys related works organized by approach and critiques each cluster. Section~\ref{sec:synthesis} synthesizes the three gaps that remain unresolved across the literature.

\section{Large Language Models}
\label{sec:llms}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

To understand how knowledge graph constraints can be applied to language models, it is first necessary to understand the underlying mechanism of language model generation. Large language models (LLMs) are the foundational engines driving modern natural language processing. This section establishes the architectural and generative properties of LLMs, specifically detailing the autoregressive process that gives rise to both their fluent reasoning capabilities and their vulnerability to factual hallucination.

\subsection{Transformer Architecture}

Modern LLMs are built upon the Transformer architecture, introduced by \citet{vaswani2017attention}. At its core, the Transformer relies on a self-attention mechanism that allows the model to dynamically weigh the importance of different tokens in an input sequence regardless of their positional distance. This represented a fundamental shift from prior recurrent architectures, enabling highly parallelized training and the capture of long-range dependencies. While the original framework proposed an encoder-decoder structure designed for translation tasks, the field has largely converged on decoder-only variants for text generation. In a decoder-only model, self-attention is causally masked, meaning that when computing the representation for a given token, the model can only attend to previous tokens and not future ones. This strictly left-to-right processing naturally aligns with the task of predicting the next word in a sequence. The Llama-3.1-8B model~\citep{dubey2024llama3}, which serves as the base generator in this project along with earlier frameworks, is precisely this kind of decoder-only, causally-masked transformer.

\subsection{Autoregressive Generation}

The text production process in these models is fundamentally autoregressive. Given a vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$, an input sequence $x$, and previously generated output tokens $y_{<t}$, the model computes a probability distribution over the entire vocabulary for the next token: $P(y_t | y_{<t}, x)$. Because the generation condition includes all previously generated tokens, every new word becomes part of the context for the next word. Decoding strategies, such as beam search and nucleus sampling~\citep{holtzman2020curious}, determine how a specific token is selected from this computed distribution. Crucially, because this distribution is always computed over the full vocabulary, any mathematically possible token has a strictly positive, non-zero probability of being generated at any step. This autoregressive property is the mechanism that produces highly fluent, coherent text; however, because each step conditions on prior outputs without an external verifier, it is also the direct structural cause of hallucination.

\subsection{Large-Scale Pretraining}

The transition from basic transformers to ``large'' language models is driven by scale in both parameter count and training data. Foundational models are pretrained on massive corpora of unlabelled text using a simple self-supervised objective, typically predicting the next token. The GPT series (\citealt{brown2020gpt3}; \citealt{achiam2023gpt4}) and the open-weight Llama series (\citealt{touvron2023llama}; \citealt{dubey2024llama3}) demonstrated that scaling this simple objective leads to the emergence of powerful downstream capabilities, including few-shot learning and broad world knowledge. During pretraining, the model internalises patterns, facts, and logical structures present in its training distribution. However, this parametric memory is fundamentally static and statistical. While scaling up models improves their fluency, syntax, and ability to recall commonly observed facts, it does not fix the underlying generation mechanism. The model still lacks a structural way to interact with dynamic external facts or guarantee that its statistical outputs map to verified truth in the real world.

\subsection{Instruction Following and Chain-of-Thought}

To align these pretrained base models with user intent, they undergo post-training processes such as instruction tuning and reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF). This alignment allows models to engage in dialogue and follow complex instructions. One of the most significant capabilities elicited from aligned LLMs is multi-step reasoning, typically prompted via Chain-of-Thought (CoT; \citealt{wei2022cot}). As surveyed by \citet{huang2023towards}, CoT prompting encourages the model to generate intermediate reasoning steps before arriving at a final answer, effectively breaking down complex problems. While CoT dramatically improves performance on logic and reasoning benchmarks, it suffers from the same autoregressive limitation: each intermediate reasoning step is generated using only the model's internal statistical distributions. Therefore, while CoT helps structure a language model's reasoning process, it cannot logically ground those outputs in verifiable sources. This persistent lack of factual grounding creates the necessity for more rigorous interventions, such as knowledge graph-constrained decoding.

\section{Hallucination in Large Language Models}
\label{sec:hallucination}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Definition and Taxonomy}

Hallucination in natural language generation refers to the production of content that is fluent and internally coherent yet factually unsupported by or in outright contradiction with verifiable sources~\citep{ji2023survey}. \citet{huang2023hallucination} refine this definition into a two-part taxonomy. \textit{Intrinsic hallucination} occurs when the model output directly contradicts material present in the provided source context. \textit{Extrinsic hallucination} occurs when the output contains claims that cannot be verified against any external source, even if not directly contradicting a specific reference.

Both types are documented at non-trivial rates in deployed systems. \citet{ji2023survey} survey over 30 works across summarization, dialogue, and question answering, finding hallucination rates ranging from 15\% to over 60\% depending on task type and measurement methodology. In the specific context of knowledge graph question answering, \citet{luo2024gcr} report that retrieval-augmented reasoning approaches produce hallucinated reasoning steps in approximately one-third of cases even when provided direct access to a supporting knowledge graph, underscoring that the problem is not simply one of insufficient external knowledge.

\subsection{Structural Causes: The Autoregressive Generation Mechanism}

The root cause of hallucination lies in the autoregressive generation mechanism shared by all modern large language models. At each generation step, the model computes a probability distribution over its vocabulary conditioned on the input and all previously generated tokens. Three structural properties of this mechanism make hallucination an ever-present risk rather than an occasional failure.

First, the generation distribution is computed entirely from learned parameters, with no term that corresponds to a factual verification operation against an external source. The model has no mechanism to ``check'' a candidate token against a knowledge base before selecting it. Second, errors propagate forward: because each step is conditioned on all prior output, a hallucinated token at step $t$ becomes part of the conditioning context for step $t{+}1$, producing fluent and internally consistent continuations of an already-incorrect chain. Third, the distribution is defined over the full vocabulary at every step, meaning that any token has a strictly positive probability of selection at any step, regardless of factual correctness~\citep{brown2020gpt3,huang2023hallucination,ji2023survey}.

\subsection{The Scaling Paradox}

A common objection to the severity of the hallucination problem is that sufficiently large models should eventually achieve adequate factual reliability. The evidence does not support this view. \citet{lin2022truthfulqa} introduce TruthfulQA, a benchmark targeting questions that frequently elicit plausible but false responses. Their central finding is that larger models do not outperform smaller models on truthfulness and in certain categories exhibit \textit{inverse scaling}; in other words, larger models perform worse because they more effectively reproduce prevalent but incorrect beliefs from training data.

\citet{perez2022sycophancy} identify a compounding problem in models trained with reinforcement learning from human feedback: such models learn to generate confident-sounding responses that align with apparent user expectations rather than with verifiable facts, decoupling confidence from accuracy. \citet{kandpal2023large} show that while larger models memorize more training-data facts and improve on frequently-seen entity combinations, they do not improve on compositional questions requiring combinations of facts not seen during training. This precisely mirrors the structure of multi-hop knowledge graph questions. \citet{mallen2023not} confirm substantial hallucination rates for GPT-4 on questions involving tail entities, regardless of model scale. Together, these findings establish that hallucination is a structural feature of the autoregressive mechanism, not a scale problem with a straightforward remedy.

\section{Multi-Step Reasoning: Chain-of-Thought and Its Limits}
\label{sec:cot}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Chain-of-Thought Prompting}

Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting, introduced by \citet{wei2022cot}, is the dominant paradigm for eliciting multi-step reasoning from large language models. By including worked examples in the prompt that show intermediate reasoning steps, the model learns to decompose complex problems into a sequence of simpler sub-problems before producing a final answer. \citet{wei2022cot} demonstrate substantial accuracy improvements on arithmetic, commonsense, and multi-hop question answering benchmarks, with the capability emerging at approximately 100 billion parameters. Zero-shot chain-of-thought~\citep{kojima2022zero} achieves similar benefits without worked examples by adding the phrase ``Let's think step by step'' to the prompt, demonstrating that the underlying capability is latent in sufficiently large models and requires only elicitation.

The limitation of CoT is that each intermediate step is generated by the same autoregressive mechanism described in the previous section. An incorrect intermediate step propagates forward as conditioning context, and no mechanism verifies that any step corresponds to a fact in an external knowledge source. A CoT chain can be entirely fluent and internally coherent while containing one or more steps that are factually fabricated.

\subsection{Extensions: Self-Consistency and Tree-of-Thought}

Two major extensions of chain-of-thought address its reliability limitations without resolving its structural faithfulness problem. Self-consistency~\citep{wang2023selfconsistency} samples multiple independent CoT chains and selects the final answer by majority vote, marginalizing over individual chain errors. This approach substantially improves accuracy on benchmarks such as GSM8K and multi-hop QA by reducing sensitivity to individual hallucinated chains. However, it provides no guarantee that any single chain is factually grounded: a majority of independently hallucinated chains can still produce an incorrect majority-vote answer, particularly on deep multi-hop questions where error probabilities compound.

Tree-of-Thought~\citep{yao2023tree} frames generation as a search over a tree of partial reasoning sequences, using the language model as both a generator and an evaluator to prune unpromising branches. This improves performance on tasks requiring backtracking and exploration. The fundamental limitation remains: pruning decisions are made by the model itself, using its own parametric knowledge as the sole evaluator, without any external verification of the factual status of candidate reasoning steps.

\subsection{The Faithfulness Ceiling of Prompting-Based Approaches}

The common limitation of all prompting-based approaches, including few-shot, chain-of-thought, self-consistency, tree-of-thought, and their combinations, is that they modify the \textit{input} to the language model without modifying the \textit{generation mechanism}. Because decoding still occurs over the full vocabulary, every token retains a strictly positive probability at every step. True structural faithfulness, defined as the guarantee that every generated token corresponds to a verified external fact, requires that invalid tokens be assigned exactly zero probability. This can only be achieved by intervening directly in the decoding algorithm, which is the function of constrained decoding approaches discussed in Section~\ref{sec:constrained}~\citep{geng2023gcd, willard2023outlines}.

\section{Knowledge Graphs and Knowledge Graph Question Answering}
\label{sec:kgqa}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Knowledge Graphs}

A knowledge graph is a structured database of verified facts, represented as a set of directed, labelled triplets $(e, r, e')$ where $e, e' \in \mathcal{E}$ are entities and $r \in \mathcal{R}$ is a relation type. Each triplet encodes a single human-verified fact: for example, (\textit{Marie Curie}, \textit{award\_received}, \textit{Nobel\_Prize\_Physics}) or (\textit{Blue\_Hawaii}, \textit{film.film.featured\_film\_location}, \textit{Hawaii}). Freebase~\citep{bollacker2008freebase}, the knowledge graph underlying the evaluation benchmarks used in this work, encodes tens of millions of such triplets across geography, history, entertainment, and science, and has been widely adopted as the standard resource for KGQA research~\citep{yih2016websqp, talmor2018cwq}.

The critical distinction between a knowledge graph and an unstructured text corpus is that every triplet in the KG has been explicitly verified and is machine-readable in a deterministic, unambiguous form. This property makes KGs suitable as sources for hard constraints on generation: a candidate token can be verified against the KG via an exact lookup, rather than via probabilistic semantic matching against raw text. A multi-hop reasoning path of length $L$ through the KG is a sequence $(e_0, r_1, e_1, \ldots, r_L, e_L)$ in which each consecutive triplet $(e_{i-1}, r_i, e_i)$ is a verified member of $\mathcal{T}$~\citep{lan2022kgqa}.

\subsection{Knowledge Graph Question Answering}

Knowledge graph question answering (KGQA) requires a system to identify the answer entity $e_L$ to a natural language question $q$ by reasoning through a chain of verified KG triplets. The task is formally defined as: given question $q$ with associated question entities $\mathcal{E}_q \subseteq \mathcal{E}$ identified by entity linking, identify the terminal entity of the correct reasoning path in $\mathcal{G}$~\citep{lan2022kgqa}.

Early KGQA approaches relied on either semantic parsing (converting questions to structured SPARQL queries) or information retrieval over KG subgraphs~\citep{lan2022kgqa}. More recent approaches use large language models as retrievers, agents iteratively querying the KG, or generators conditioned on retrieved subgraph context~\citep{jiang2023structgpt, jin2024graphcot, luo2024rog}. The shift toward LLM-based KGQA has improved fluency and coverage but reintroduced the hallucination risk that structured approaches avoided: LLM-generated reasoning chains are not guaranteed to correspond to verified KG paths.

\subsection{Evaluation Benchmarks}

Two benchmark datasets provide the evaluation setting for KGQA research. WebQSP~\citep{yih2016websqp} is derived from the WebQuestions dataset and comprises 4,737 questions grounded in Freebase with reasoning depths of one to two hops, annotated with SPARQL queries and evaluated using Hits@1 and F1. ComplexWebQuestions (CWQ;~\citealt{talmor2018cwq}) extends WebQSP by compositionally transforming questions through conjunction, composition, and comparative operations, yielding approximately 34,000 questions requiring up to four reasoning hops. WebQSP represents a regime where the set of valid paths is comparatively small; CWQ represents the regime where valid path sets grow large and the difficulty of identifying the correct path among structurally valid alternatives is highest.

\subsection{Multi-Hop Complexity}

The combinatorial difficulty of multi-hop KGQA increases sharply with hop depth. For an entity with average out-degree $d$ in the KG, the number of valid paths of length $L$ reachable from the question entities grows as $|\mathcal{E}_q| \times d^L$. For well-connected Freebase entities where $d \approx 20$ and a question with up to three question entities, a three-hop question can produce up to 24{,}000 structurally valid paths, of which exactly one leads to the correct answer~\citep{he2021multihop, lan2022kgqa}. This combinatorial explosion has concrete consequences for any approach that must discriminate among candidate paths: at each generation step, the model must select the correct next token from among a very large set of valid-but-incorrect alternatives, relying on its parametric knowledge to do so.

\section{Constrained Decoding for Faithful Text Generation}
\label{sec:constrained}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Constrained decoding is the paradigm in which generation is restricted to a predefined valid set at each step through direct intervention in the decoding mechanism. Unlike prompting, which modifies the input to the LLM, constrained decoding modifies the token selection process itself: invalid tokens are assigned zero probability before sampling, making their generation structurally impossible.

\subsection{Logit Masking}

The foundational mechanism of constrained decoding is logit masking, described formally in \citet{geng2023gcd} and \citet{willard2023outlines}. A constraint oracle computes a binary validity mask over the vocabulary at each step; the mask is applied element-wise to the raw logit vector before softmax normalization, setting invalid token logits to negative infinity and thereby assigning them zero probability. Token selection then operates exclusively over the valid subset. The central design question in any constrained decoding system is how the validity mask is computed: what information the oracle uses, and how it changes across generation steps.

\subsection{Grammar-Constrained Decoding}

\citet{geng2023gcd} introduce Grammar-Constrained Decoding (GCD), which constrains generation to strings conforming to a context-free grammar, using an Earley parser as the oracle. The valid next-token set at each step is the set of tokens that extend the current partial string as a valid grammar prefix. GCD additionally introduces input-dependent grammars, in which the grammar is parameterized by the input question, representing a partial step toward context-sensitive constraints. \citet{willard2023outlines} address the computational cost of constrained decoding with an efficient finite-state machine formulation. By precomputing an index from automaton states to valid vocabulary subsets, the valid token set can be retrieved in amortized constant time at each generation step, making constrained decoding practical at inference speed.

The shared limitation of these format-constraint approaches is that they enforce structural validity of the output format (such as grammatical correctness or schema conformance) without enforcing factual correctness. A grammatically valid output can contain entirely hallucinated facts. The transition from format constraints to factual constraints requires a constraint oracle that verifies tokens against a knowledge source rather than against a grammar.

\subsection{Knowledge Graph-Constrained Decoding}

The application of constrained decoding to knowledge graph faithfulness is a more recent development. The core idea is to use the KG itself as the constraint oracle: at each generation step, only tokens that correspond to valid continuations of a KG reasoning path are admitted. This approach guarantees structural faithfulness, meaning every generated token corresponds to a verified KG triplet, which makes hallucination of KG facts structurally impossible.

\textbf{Graph-Constrained Reasoning (GCR)}~\citep{luo2024gcr} is the state of the art in this line of work. GCR constructs a KG-Trie (a prefix tree over all KG paths within $L$ hops of the question entities, built by breadth-first search before generation begins) and uses it as the constraint oracle during beam-search decoding. The trie is queried at each generation step to return valid next tokens. GCR achieves 92.6 Hits@1 on WebQSP and 75.8 on CWQ with verified 100\% structural faithfulness using a Llama-3.1-8B backbone. A key property is that the trie is constructed once before generation and never modified: the valid token set is fixed regardless of what has been generated.

\textbf{Decoding on Graphs (DoG)}~\citep{li2024dog} introduces step-wise trie expansion as a partial remedy to GCR's static oracle. DoG defines \textit{well-formed chains} as sequences of KG triplets in which every triplet exists in the KG and every entity is connected to the preceding triplet or is a question entity. It enforces this definition through step-wise expansion: after committing to an entity, the constraint trie is extended with all KG neighbors of that entity. This makes the oracle topologically dynamic, growing as reasoning progresses through the graph. DoG achieves 90.99 Hits@1 on WebQSP and 76.08 on CWQ with 100\% structural faithfulness, and is training-free, requiring no fine-tuning. However, the expansion rule admits all KG neighbors of each visited entity regardless of their relevance to the question or the current reasoning direction; and per-step trie reconstruction sacrifices the efficiency of precomputed constraint structures.

\textbf{RouterKGQA}~\citep{yuan2026routerkgqa} takes a different approach, routing between a specialised KG-reasoning model and a general LLM based on question complexity. It applies semantic filtering using SBERT embeddings (specifically nomic-embed-text-v1) to select among candidate answers after generation, achieving competitive accuracy with reduced inference cost. RouterKGQA's semantic scoring operates at the answer selection level rather than at the token constraint level: paths are generated first and filtered retrospectively, rather than being constrained during generation.

\subsection{Beam Search and Trie-Based Constraints}

To understand how knowledge graph constraints are practically enforced during generation, it is essential to examine the two primary search algorithms and data structures involved: beam search and the prefix trie.

\textbf{Beam Search.} First introduced in the context of the HARPY speech recognition system~\citep{lowerre1976harpy}, beam search is a heuristic search algorithm used in natural language processing to decode sequences from probabilistic models. Unlike greedy decoding, which exclusively selects the single most probable token at each step and risks generating sub-optimal global sequences, beam search maintains a set of the $k$ most promising partial hypotheses (or ``beams") at any given time. At each generation step, the algorithm expands all $k$ current hypotheses by scoring their possible continuations, ranks the resulting sequences based on their cumulative log-probability, and prunes the search space back down to the top $k$ candidates~\citep{holtzman2020curious}. This approach mitigates the risk of early compounding errors while remaining computationally tractable, making it the dominant decoding strategy for tasks requiring structured and coherent output.

\textbf{The Prefix Trie.} A trie, originating from the concept of a ``trie memory" (retrieval) introduced by \citet{fredkin1960trie}, is a deterministic tree-based data structure used to store a dynamic set of sequences, typically strings. In a trie, no single node stores a complete string; instead, a node's position in the tree defines the exact prefix it represents. The branches extending from any given node represent the deterministic set of valid next characters or tokens. In the domain of constrained decoding, a pre-computed trie serves as a highly efficient constraint oracle. Given the current generated sequence prefix, traversing the trie to the corresponding node yields the exact subset of valid next tokens in amortised constant time, $O(1)$, relative to vocabulary size. This operation is critically more efficient than running continuous dynamic parser evaluations, making trie lookups the most scalable pattern for enforcing hard factual constraints at inference speed~\citep{willard2023outlines}.

\textbf{The KG-Trie.} For knowledge graph-constrained generation, architectures such as Graph-Constrained Reasoning (GCR) utilize a specialized adaptation known as the KG-Trie~\citep{luo2024gcr}. A KG-Trie serializes structurally verified multi-hop paths from the knowledge graph into string formats and maps them across the language model's precise token vocabulary. Rather than nodes representing single alphabetical characters, each edge in the KG-Trie represents a specific valid token in the LLM's vast vocabulary space.

When beam search is merged with a KG-Trie oracle (as visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:kg_trie_search}), the decoding process transforms into a strictly bounded traversal of the prefix tree. At each autoregressive step, the current beam hypotheses map to specific states in the KG-Trie. The language model calculates a probability distribution over its entire vocabulary, but a mathematical mask forces the probability of any candidate token not validly present as a child node in the trie to exactly zero ($-\infty$ prior to softmax normalization). The beam search then advances strictly down the permissible branches, functionally restricting the LLM's autoregressive generation to a set of pre-verified deterministic paths while still allowing the model to express internal preference (via log-probabilities) among those factually valid chains.

\section{Retrieval-Augmented Generation}
\label{sec:rag}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), introduced by \citet{lewis2020rag}, is the dominant paradigm for grounding LLM outputs in external knowledge. A dense retriever selects relevant passages from an external source based on embedding similarity, and a reader LLM generates the answer conditioned on the retrieved context and the question. Fusion-in-Decoder~\citep{izacard2021leveraging} extends this by encoding multiple retrieved passages independently and fusing them in the decoder, improving coverage.

\subsection{KG-Augmented Retrieval}

Several works augment RAG with KG-structured context. \citet{he2021multihop} retrieve KG subgraphs and use attention over retrieved facts to guide multi-hop reasoning. \citet{yasunaga2021qagnn} combine retrieved text passages and KG neighborhoods via graph neural networks. \citet{shi2021kgpath} retrieve KG paths as natural language context strings, providing path-shaped input to the reader. G-Retriever~\citep{he2024gretriever} applies RAG specifically to textual graph understanding, retrieving graph-structured context for QA tasks.

\subsection{Advanced RAG Variants}

More recent RAG variants address the timing and precision of retrieval. Self-RAG~\citep{asai2024selfrag} inserts reflection tokens that allow the model to dynamically decide when retrieval is needed and whether retrieved passages support the claim being generated, improving retrieval precision and citation accuracy. FLARE~\citep{jiang2023flare} uses the model's next-token prediction confidence as a retrieval trigger, retrieving fresh context when confidence falls below a threshold. IRCoT~\citep{trivedi2022ircot} interleaves retrieval with chain-of-thought steps, retrieving relevant context at each intermediate reasoning step and improving multi-hop accuracy substantially. GraphRAG~\citep{edge2024graphrag} constructs community-structured knowledge graphs from document corpora and retrieves community summaries for complex relational queries, explicitly leveraging graph structure for improved coverage.

\subsection{The Structural Limitation of Soft Grounding}

Despite their empirical improvements, all RAG approaches share a structural limitation: retrieved context is a soft influence on generation. The retriever determines what the LLM sees; it does not determine what the LLM is permitted to generate. Because the LLM's generation mechanism remains unchanged (the distribution over the full vocabulary is computed autoregressively), there exists a nonzero probability of generating any token at any step, including tokens that correspond to no verified KG fact. Structural faithfulness with probability one, which guarantees that every generated token corresponds to a verified fact, cannot be achieved under any architecture that modifies only the input context without modifying the token selection mechanism~\citep{luo2024gcr, geng2023gcd}.

\section{Semantic Relevance Scoring with Sentence Transformers}
\label{sec:sbert}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Sentence Transformer Embeddings}

Sentence-BERT~\citep{reimers2019sbert} introduced a class of sentence encoders trained to produce dense vector representations in which semantically similar sentences cluster together and semantically dissimilar sentences are pushed apart. The SBERT architecture applies mean pooling over token representations from a BERT-like transformer encoder, producing a fixed-length embedding. Training uses a siamese network on labelled sentence pairs with a classification objective over entailment, neutral, and contradiction labels. The resulting embedding space supports cosine similarity as a reliable proxy for semantic alignment between sentences, without requiring task-specific fine-tuning at inference time.

\subsection{Applications in Retrieval and Graph Reasoning}

Sentence transformer similarity has been applied extensively in dense retrieval settings. \citet{karpukhin2020dpr} demonstrate that a bi-encoder architecture trained on question-passage pairs produces embeddings that outperform sparse retrieval (BM25) on open-domain QA. \citet{xiong2020adore} refine bi-encoder training, improving coverage of relevant passages. In the KGQA context, \citet{shi2021kgpath} and \citet{saxena2022kgqa} apply semantic similarity to rank candidate KG paths for retrieval, selecting high-scoring paths as soft context for the reader LLM. These approaches use cosine similarity as a soft ranking signal over candidate paths, selecting the top-$k$ for context provision.

\subsection{Encoder Architecture Trade-offs}

The choice of sentence encoder involves a quality-latency tradeoff that is particularly stringent in step-wise constrained decoding, where the encoder is invoked at each generation step. Larger encoders such as all-mpnet-base-v2 (110M parameters) and BGE-M3~\citep{chen2024bge} (568M parameters) achieve higher performance on semantic textual similarity benchmarks but incur substantially higher inference latency. Lexical approaches such as BM25 are fast but cannot capture semantic similarity between paraphrase-equivalent paths that share no surface tokens. The all-MiniLM-L6-v2 model~\citep{reimers2019sbert}, at 22M parameters with approximately 80ms per-query CPU latency, provides the optimal balance of semantic quality and inference speed for iterative step-wise applications.

\section{Related Works}
\label{sec:related}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The following sections organise prior work into thematic clusters, describing what each cluster of approaches achieves, the shared mechanisms across works within each cluster, and the limitations that motivate further research.

\subsection{Knowledge Graph-Enhanced LLM Reasoning}

A large body of work explores how LLMs can be enhanced with KG access for reasoning and question answering without enforcing structural faithfulness constraints. \citet{jiang2023structgpt} introduce StructGPT, a general framework enabling LLMs to reason over structured data including KGs through an iterative reading-and-reasoning procedure. \citet{jin2024graphcot} propose Graph Chain-of-Thought, augmenting LLM CoT reasoning with graph-structured evidence retrieved at each reasoning step. \citet{luo2024rog} introduce Reasoning on Graphs (RoG), which generates reasoning plans over KG paths and executes them faithfully, achieving strong multi-hop accuracy while maintaining path interpretability. \citet{jiang2022unikgqa} propose UniKGQA, unifying retrieval and reasoning for multi-hop KGQA through a shared parameter space.

These works collectively demonstrate the value of explicit KG access for improving LLM reasoning quality and interpretability. Their shared limitation is that KG access is advisory rather than constraining: the LLM attends to retrieved KG context but is free to generate beyond it. The reasoning chains produced may be plausible and KG-informed without being guaranteed to correspond to verified KG paths. \citet{luo2024gcr} measure this gap directly, finding that RoG-style approaches produce hallucinated reasoning steps in approximately 33\% of cases despite explicit KG access.

\subsection{Retrieval-Based KGQA}

Earlier KGQA approaches relied on structured retrieval rather than LLM generation. \citet{he2021multihop} propose a method for multi-hop KGQA that learns intermediate supervision signals to guide path selection through the KG, showing that explicit intermediate supervision improves performance at greater hop depths. \citet{yasunaga2021qagnn} combine text and KG context in a joint graph neural network, propagating information between retrieved passage tokens and KG entity nodes. \citet{shi2021kgpath} retrieve KG paths by semantic similarity and provide them as natural language context to a reader model. \citet{saxena2022kgqa} apply entity and relation embeddings to select multi-hop paths for KGQA without LLM generation.

These retrieval-based approaches avoid LLM hallucination by operating within the structured KG rather than generating freely. Their limitation is expressiveness: they are typically constrained to fixed path structures and do not leverage the multi-step reasoning capability of modern LLMs. The transition to LLM-based approaches improved naturalness and coverage at the cost of faithfulness guarantees.

\subsection{Format-Constrained and Entity-Constrained Generation}

\citet{decao2021autoregressive} introduce autoregressive entity retrieval, constraining generation to a finite set of entity names using a trie over known entity strings. This approach ensures that generated entities correspond to real entities in a fixed vocabulary but does not enforce path-level faithfulness across multiple steps. \citet{geng2023gcd} generalise this to arbitrary context-free grammar constraints, while \citet{willard2023outlines} provide an efficient finite-state automaton formulation. \citet{deutsch2019constrained} propose a general algorithm for constrained sequential inference applicable across structured prediction tasks.

These works establish that hard constraints on generation are computationally tractable and empirically beneficial. Their limitation for KGQA is that they enforce output \textit{format} rather than \textit{factual content}: constraining generation to grammatically valid strings does not ensure that the strings correspond to verified KG paths. The extension from format constraints to KG path constraints requires a constraint oracle that encodes the relational structure of the knowledge graph.

\subsection{Semantic Similarity in Graph Reasoning}

Several works apply semantic similarity signals to guide reasoning over knowledge graphs. \citet{shi2021kgpath} rank candidate KG paths by semantic similarity between the question and path descriptions, retrieving high-scoring paths as evidence. \citet{saxena2022kgqa} learn embeddings for KG entities and relations that support similarity-based multi-hop path selection. \citet{he2021multihop} use attention-guided traversal over retrieved KG subgraphs, with attention weights reflecting the relevance of each subgraph region to the question. PathRetriever~\citep{asai2019learning} learns to retrieve reasoning paths from evidence graphs by scoring paths using a recurrent model over their node sequences.

These works demonstrate that semantic similarity can effectively differentiate relevant from irrelevant KG paths in a retrieval setting. The key architectural distinction is that semantic similarity is applied as a \textit{soft ranking signal}: candidate paths are scored and the highest-scoring ones are provided to the LLM as context or used to select among generated candidates. No existing work applies semantic similarity as a \textit{hard binary admission criterion} that determines which tokens the LLM is permitted to generate at each decoding step. The distinction between soft retrieval signals and hard generation constraints is significant: the former influences what the LLM sees, while the latter determines what the LLM can produce.

\subsection{Hallucination Mitigation in Knowledge-Intensive Tasks}

Beyond the constrained decoding paradigm, a range of approaches attempt to reduce hallucination in knowledge-intensive tasks. Retrieval-augmented approaches (including RAG~\citep{lewis2020rag}, Self-RAG~\citep{asai2024selfrag}, FLARE~\citep{jiang2023flare}, IRCoT~\citep{trivedi2022ircot}, and GraphRAG~\citep{edge2024graphrag}) address the problem by providing relevant external context, improving factual coverage without modifying the generation mechanism. \citet{wang2023selfconsistency} improve consistency by majority-voting over multiple CoT chains. \citet{huang2024selfcorrect} study whether LLMs can self-correct reasoning errors, finding that self-correction is generally ineffective without external feedback, reinforcing that internal parametric knowledge cannot reliably detect its own errors.

Post-hoc verification approaches~\citep{nakano2021webgpt, gao2023rarr} check generated claims against external sources after generation and revise or flag unsupported statements. While effective at identifying errors, these approaches require a separate verification pass and do not prevent hallucination during generation. \citet{agrawal2024mindful} systematically analyse failure modes in RAG systems, finding that even with relevant context provided, LLMs regularly produce statements unsupported by the retrieved evidence.

\subsection{Concurrent Work}

RouterKGQA~\citep{yuan2026routerkgqa} is the work most closely related to this research in terms of shared components. RouterKGQA routes KGQA queries between a specialised KG reasoning model and a general LLM based on question complexity, and applies SBERT-based semantic similarity (using the nomic-embed-text-v1 encoder) to filter candidate reasoning paths. It achieves competitive performance with reduced inference cost, averaging approximately 1.15 LLM calls per question.

The architectural distinction from the approach in this dissertation is the level at which semantic scoring is applied. RouterKGQA scores complete reasoning paths after they have been generated, using similarity to filter among candidates retrospectively. This approach does not modify the token selection mechanism during generation: the LLM generates freely, and semantic similarity is applied post-hoc to select among the outputs. In the constrained decoding paradigm, by contrast, semantic similarity would need to operate as a hard admission criterion applied before or during token selection, determining which tokens the LLM is permitted to generate at each step. RouterKGQA does not provide a metric for constraint permissiveness and does not measure the degree to which its candidate sets contain structurally valid but semantically irrelevant paths.

\section{Synthesis: Three Unresolved Gaps}
\label{sec:synthesis}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The preceding review reveals that two distinct research trajectories (constrained decoding for structural faithfulness, and semantic similarity for relevance-guided reasoning) have developed largely in parallel without being integrated at the constraint-oracle level. Constrained decoding approaches~\citep{luo2024gcr, li2024dog} achieve structural faithfulness but construct their constraint oracles without reference to question semantics or generation context, producing valid token sets that contain large numbers of irrelevant paths. Semantic similarity approaches~\citep{shi2021kgpath, saxena2022kgqa, yuan2026routerkgqa} improve relevance but operate as soft signals that influence retrieval or post-hoc selection without modifying the generation mechanism.

Three specific gaps emerge from this review.

\textbf{Gap 1 -- The Permissiveness Problem.} Existing KG-constrained decoding frameworks construct their constraint oracles as functions of graph structure and question entities alone, without reference to question semantics or the evolving generation state. For multi-hop questions, this produces valid token sets containing thousands of structurally correct but semantically irrelevant paths at every generation step. The LLM must discriminate among these paths using its own parametric knowledge, reintroducing the hallucination risk that constrained decoding was designed to eliminate~\citep{luo2024gcr, li2024dog}.

\textbf{Gap 2 -- The Static-Dynamic Efficiency Tradeoff.} GCR's precomputed static trie is computationally efficient but semantically blind and identical at every generation step. DoG's step-wise topological expansion introduces dynamism at the cost of per-step trie reconstruction and without semantic filtering~\citep{li2024dog, willard2023outlines}. No existing framework achieves semantic responsiveness (where the valid set narrows progressively as reasoning commits to a specific direction) while preserving the efficiency of precomputed constraint structures.

\textbf{Gap 3 -- The Absence of a Constraint Quality Metric.} No existing work provides a metric that quantifies constraint permissiveness independently of downstream answer accuracy. Without such a metric, it is impossible to measure progress on reducing irrelevant paths in the valid set, compare frameworks on constraint quality independently of model quality, or diagnose permissiveness as a failure mode in deployed systems~\citep{luo2024gcr, li2024dog}.
